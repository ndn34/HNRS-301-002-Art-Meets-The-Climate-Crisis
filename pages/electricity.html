Discussion

Today, most electronics are actually very energy efficient. Many of these values are difficult to determine and take some of the higher estimates. That is not to say these values are incorrect, but they may be overcompensating. Your individual usage really doesn't add much. The big issue are the massive data centers around the world. According to Pew Research, data centers in the US used nearly 200 terawatt hours of electricity in 2024. A number that is likely much higher in 2025, and will likely increase again in 2026. If you are wondering how AI Chatbots can use so much water, the answer is the data center. According to the Environmental and Energy Study Institute, a singular data center can consume upwards 5 million gallons of water a day. Which is equivalent to the consumption of water by a town populated by 10,000 to 50,000 people. There are over 4,000 data centers in the United States currently. This is where you get those numbers about AI Chatbots from. However, it is important to note that it is not just AI Chatbots using these data centers. Most of the internet is hosted on these. Data storage and general automation are actually the top users of these centers. Of course, things like AI and streaming add to them as well. AI specifically is growing at a pace that necessitates, for them, an increase in the amount and capabilities of these centers. Companies have worked hard to increase the efficiency of the electronics we use daily. However, the backbones of that technology are horrific in their stature. It is a gap that is scary to imagine, and something we think more people should be aware of.

"Anatomy of an AI System" by Kate Crawford and Vladan Joler


This is Anatomy of an AI System by Kate Crawford and Vladan Joler. You can view it in higher quality here https://anatomyof.ai/. There on that page is also a more in depth look at everything that goes into these systems. It is also important to note that this was made in 2018, before the big AI bubble we live in now. I noted earlier how what we interact with upfront is very optimised and doesn't take much. However, the inner workings behind the scene are massive and destructive. This image showcases much of what I meant. There is an absurd amount that goes into these project. Way more than we think of on the surface. This site may be called You Are The Anthropocene, but I think it is also important to note how the actions of a population are not the only thing impacting the world. Industry is a major contributor as well. The page is a long read, but I think it is worth a skim at the very least. I love how they broke the image down into many different graphs. I love how it all adds into this big line that shows how everything flows together. The lines in the middle are reminiscent of oil pipes to me. I think it is intentional since the other lines are substantially thinner. Crawford and Joler's work shows that climate change and environmental impact are not limited to just what we interact with immediately. There are many costs hidden to us that we are complacent with since it is not immediately obvious to us. We need to keep up with technology and ensure that we rein it's impact as much as we can.
